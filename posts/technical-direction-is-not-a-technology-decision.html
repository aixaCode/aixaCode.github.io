Technical Direction Is Not a Technology Decision

Jun 20, 2026

One thing I've noticed over the years is that engineering teams love talking about technology.

Should we move to microservices? Should we rewrite the system? Should we adopt a new framework? Should we build our own solution?

I've been part of many of those discussions myself. They are important discussions to have.

But looking back, I think many of them started from the wrong place.

The best technical decisions I've seen were rarely driven by technology itself. They were driven by a clear understanding of where the company wanted to go.

When that direction is clear, technology choices become much easier.

When it isn't, teams can spend months debating solutions without ever agreeing on the actual problem.

I learned this most clearly during the rebuild of Decentraland's client.

From the outside, it looked like a large technology project. We rebuilt the client from the ground up.

What is interesting is that we didn't change the engine.

The old client was built in Unity. The new client was built in Unity as well.

If the goal had been choosing better technology, that would be a strange outcome.

The real goal was improving the experience for users.

We wanted larger social events. Better graphics. More stable performance. More people sharing the same space without everything falling apart.

Once we defined the outcome we were after, many technical decisions became surprisingly obvious.

Some parts were worth replacing. Some parts were worth keeping.

The technology followed the strategy, not the other way around.

Since then, I've started looking at technical direction differently.

Whenever a discussion becomes focused on tools, frameworks, or architecture, I usually try to step back and ask a different question:

What are we actually trying to achieve?

Most of the time, the answer to that question tells us far more than another architecture diagram ever will.
