Prioritization Is Constraint Management

Jun 20, 2026

For a long time, I thought prioritization was about finding the best idea.

The longer I've worked in engineering leadership, the more I've realized that's usually not the problem.

Most teams don't suffer from a lack of good ideas.

They suffer from having too many.

Performance improvements. Technical debt. New features. Infrastructure. Developer experience. QA automation. Better onboarding. Better analytics. Creator tooling.

Most of them are valuable. Most of them will eventually need to happen.

The hard part is deciding what matters now.

One thing that helped me think about prioritization differently was realizing that every organization has a bottleneck.

There is always something limiting progress.

Sometimes it's acquisition. Sometimes it's retention. Sometimes it's stability. Sometimes it's delivery speed. Sometimes it's simply not having enough people.

The challenge is identifying what that constraint is and focusing on it long enough to make a meaningful difference.

I've seen teams spend months optimizing parts of the system that were already working reasonably well while ignoring the thing that was actually holding the business back.

I've made that mistake myself.

At Decentraland we constantly had more opportunities than capacity.

We could improve performance. We could invest in creator tooling. We could modernize infrastructure. We could improve QA automation. We could improve publishing workflows.

All of those things mattered.

The difficult part wasn't deciding whether they were good ideas.

The difficult part was understanding which one was the limiting factor at that particular moment.

A few years ago, we experimented with a more structured approach to prioritization.

We collected initiatives from across the organization and scored them against a set of criteria: strategic alignment, expected impact, effort, risk, dependencies and team capacity.

On paper, it looked great.

Everyone understood the process. The scoring was transparent. The decisions felt objective.

And to be fair, it solved a lot of problems. Discussions became less emotional and more data driven.

But over time I noticed something interesting.

The framework was very good at telling us what fit within our available capacity.

It was much less effective at telling us what was actually most important.

Projects with a clear scope, lower risk and predictable delivery tended to score well.

Initiatives tackling fundamental business problems often carried more uncertainty. They were harder to estimate, harder to shape and harder to fit neatly into a planning cycle.

As a result, the framework sometimes pushed us toward the most achievable work rather than the most impactful work.

Everything looked rational on paper. The spreadsheet made sense. The scores made sense. The roadmap made sense.

Yet sometimes the most important problem remained untouched.

I saw a similar tension when using Shape Up.

There are many things I genuinely like about the methodology.

The focus on appetite instead of estimates. The emphasis on betting rather than commitments. The discipline of shaping work before handing it to teams.

Those are valuable ideas.

But like every framework, it has trade-offs.

If you're not careful, the conversation becomes focused on shaping work into something deliverable rather than understanding whether that work addresses the biggest constraint facing the business.

Teams become very efficient at producing output. Projects get completed. Cycles get closed. Roadmaps move forward.

Yet the underlying bottleneck remains exactly where it was.

Looking back, I think we sometimes spent too much time asking:

"Can we fit this into the next cycle?"

and not enough time asking:

"If we solve this, what actually changes for our users or the business?"

One example that stuck with me was support and bug management.

At one point we shifted some capacity away from support-related work and toward feature delivery and events.

Looking at it in isolation, it seemed reasonable. Features were important. Events were important. Nothing looked obviously wrong.

A few weeks later, the bug backlog started growing rapidly.

The product hadn't suddenly become worse. The team hadn't become less capable.

We had simply stopped investing in the thing that was constraining us.

The system behaved exactly as expected.

That experience taught me that prioritization decisions don't disappear just because we don't explicitly make them.

If we don't choose where capacity goes, the system chooses for us.

And usually the feedback arrives later, when it's much more expensive to fix.

Another lesson I've learned is that the best priority often changes.

A year ago, performance might have been the biggest problem. Six months later it might be creator adoption. A few months after that it might be operational scalability.

The answer changes because the system changes.

That's why I tend to be skeptical of fixed roadmaps that pretend priorities are static.

The work that creates the most value is often whatever removes the biggest constraint today.

When I look at a proposal now, I rarely start with "Is this a good idea?" Instead I ask:

"What problem is this solving?" "What is currently limiting us?" "What becomes possible if we do this?" "What happens if we don't?"

Those questions usually lead to much better conversations.

I've also noticed something interesting about experienced leaders.

The best ones I've worked with are not necessarily better at generating ideas. They are better at identifying bottlenecks.

They have a clearer understanding of where the organization is stuck and what needs to move first.

That's ultimately how I think about prioritization today.

Not as a ranking exercise. Not as a scoring framework. Not as a battle between competing projects.

Prioritization is the ongoing process of identifying the most important constraint and helping the organization move through it.

Everything else is secondary.
