How I lead engineering teams

I want teams to make good decisions without waiting for me.

Earlier in my career, I sometimes mistook being involved in every important decision for being useful. That works for a small team. As the team grows, it becomes a bottleneck.

A large part of my job now is making sure people understand the problem, have enough context and know which decisions are theirs to make. I am direct about expectations and I would rather discuss a difficult tradeoff early than discover it at the end of a project.

How I work

Start with the result

Shipping is important, but finishing a roadmap is not enough. I want to know what improved for users, creators or the business.

Say the difficult thing early

Unclear expectations rarely become clearer on their own. I try to name the problem and discuss the tradeoff while there is still time to act.

Give people real decisions

Delegation needs context and authority. If every important choice still comes back to me, I have only delegated the work.

Add process for a reason

I add a process when a recurring failure shows that we need one. A new meeting does not fix unclear ownership.

Stay close without taking over

I remain involved in technical direction, but the people closest to the system should usually choose how to build it.

Make myself less necessary

I consider it progress when managers, leads and teams can handle a familiar class of decision without my help.

When the proxy becomes the goal

It is tempting to use the thing that is easiest to see. When impact takes time, we count output. When a decision feels risky, we add an approval. When nobody is sure who owns something, we schedule another meeting.

I have done all of these. Sometimes they help. The problem starts when the temporary proxy becomes what the team is expected to optimize.

I try to check regularly whether a metric or process is still helping us, or whether we are now maintaining it out of habit.

Process replacing clarityNew meetings, templates or approval flows appear before the failure mode is named.Is this a process problem, an ownership problem or a clarity problem?
Frameworks replacing thinkingThe team optimizes for the cycle, appetite, ticket, review or status update instead of the product outcome.Is the framework creating better judgment, or just a new scoreboard?

Growing the engineering organization

One team I led had grown faster than its way of working. Too many decisions returned to the same senior people, managers lacked a clear remit and QA was often involved late. I had contributed to that dependency by staying too close to too many decisions.

We clarified ownership, gave managers and QA leads more authority and made release quality easier to see. The change took time. It involved coaching, clearer expectations and occasionally letting someone make a decision differently from how I would have made it.

The result I wanted was simple: fewer decisions waiting for the same person and more people able to carry responsibility from beginning to end.

We used frameworks where they helped, including Shape Up. None of them removed the need to set priorities, share context and trust people with consequential decisions.

Changes I have led

  • Release discipline. Moved client delivery toward predictable weekly releases with clearer QA gates, crash and hang visibility, and release confidence based on evidence.
  • Engineering health metrics. Built dashboards and scheduled agents around flow, quality, review distribution, Sentry signals, Unity Cloud costs and team load, with definitions pinned so trends mean something.
  • AI in the operating model. Introduced AI-assisted review, QA support, knowledge access and technical audits where the output changes a decision instead of adding noise.
  • Ownership and simplification. Reduced ambiguity across teams and systems by making ownership explicit, archiving stale repositories and removing obsolete technical paths.
  • Career and performance clarity. Made expectations more explicit for engineers, managers and senior ICs so feedback is tied to behavior, judgment and outcomes rather than visibility.

Things I try to avoid

  • Heroics as a habit. Rescue can be necessary, but if the same rescue repeats, the system is teaching dependency.
  • Agreement mistaken for alignment. Strong teams should be able to challenge weak assumptions without turning disagreement into politics.
  • Metrics without a behavior check. A metric is useful only if the behavior it rewards is the behavior we actually want.
  • Methodology as a substitute for judgment. Shape Up, Scrum, Kanban and OKRs can help, but none of them decide what matters or create trust for you.
  • Comfortable ambiguity. Softness can look empathetic while making the real problem harder to act on.
